*This is a real story shared with permission. Some identifying details have been changed.*

Sarah Chen opened Bread & Co from her Brunswick kitchen in March 2020. Two loaves of sourdough per day, sold to neighbours via Instagram DMs. By late 2024, the business was generating over $2 million in annual revenue, employing 9 people, and delivering to 800+ households and 40 cafés across Melbourne every week.

She did it without a single investor or business loan.

## The First Year Was Miserable

"I gave my entire pricing strategy at the beginning," Sarah told us. "I was charging $10 for a sourdough loaf because I was scared people wouldn't buy. After 3 months, I was working 70-hour weeks and barely covering my ingredients."

The turning point was a simple cost-of-goods-sold exercise. When she mapped out the real cost per loaf — including her time at minimum wage — each loaf was costing her $14 to produce. **She was losing $4 on every sale.**

She raised prices. Lost 30% of her customers overnight. Gained them back within a month.

## The Operations Lever

Sarah credits most of her growth to systems, not marketing.

- She built a **weekly production schedule** and never deviated from it.
- She introduced **a subscription model** — 200 subscribers paying $65/week for a fixed delivery box. That's $13,000 in guaranteed weekly revenue before baking a single loaf.
- She hired her first employee — a baker — before she was "ready," because capacity was the only thing capping her growth.

## What She'd Do Differently

"I'd register for GST on day one of hitting the threshold, not 6 months late. I'd also set up business banking immediately instead of mixing personal and business money."

Her biggest regret? Not understanding superannuation obligations when she hired her first staff member. "I ended up with a $12,000 super bill I wasn't ready for."

## The Lesson

Growth without systems is just chaos. Sarah's success wasn't a viral TikTok moment — it was consistent, boring, disciplined operations and pricing done correctly from the start.

There's nothing glamorous about it. There rarely is.
